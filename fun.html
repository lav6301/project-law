FUNDAMENTAL RIGHTS AND DUTIES

Fundamental Rights and duties


Fundamental Rights


These are a set of rights that are considered essential for the well-being and dignity of individuals. They are enshrined in the constitution to safeguard the interests and liberties of citizens. Fundamental Rights ensure that every individual, regardless of race, religion, gender, or any other characteristic, is treated fairly and with dignity by the state. They include rights like freedom of speech, equality before the law, and protection from discrimination. Seven main fundamental rights were originally provided by the Constitution
  • right to equality
  • right to freedom
  • right against exploitation
  • right to freedom of religion
  • cultural and educational rights
  • right to property
  • right to constitutional remedies.

Fundamental Rights are defined as basic human freedoms where every Indian citizen has the right to enjoy for a proper and harmonious development of personality and life. These rights apply universally to all citizens of India, irrespective of their race, place of birth, religion, caste or gender. They are enforceable by the courts, subject to certain restrictions.


Fundatemental duties


Fundamental Duties are moral and civic obligations that citizens are expected to follow for the betterment of society and the nation as a whole. While not legally enforceable, they are considered essential for the holistic development of the country.Fundamental Duties refer to a set of moral and civic obligations that citizens are expected to follow for the betterment of society and the nation as a whole. These duties are outlined in the Constitution of India and were added by the 42nd Amendment in 1976. While not legally enforceable by the courts, they are considered essential for the holistic development of the country.

Purpose of Fundamental Duties:


Balancing Rights with Responsibilities:

They remind citizens that while they have rights, they also have responsibilities towards society and the nation.

Promoting Social Cohesion:

They encourage a sense of unity, fraternity, and a common purpose among citizens.

Preservation of National Integrity:

They emphasize the importance of preserving the unity and integrity of the nation.

Types of Fundamental Duties:


  • To abide by the Constitution and respect its ideals and institutions.

  • To promote harmony and the spirit of common brotherhood amongst all the people of India.

  • To cherish and follow the noble ideals which inspired our national struggle for freedom.

  • To uphold and protect the sovereignty, unity, and integrity of India.

  • To defend the country and render national service when called upon to do so.

  • To renounce practices derogatory to the dignity of women.

  • To value and preserve the rich heritage of our composite culture.
  • To protect and improve the natural environment including forests, lakes, rivers, and wildlife.

  • To develop the scientific temper, humanism, and the spirit of inquiry and reform.

  • To safeguard public property and to abjure violence.

  • To strive towards excellence in all spheres of individual and collective activity.

SEVEN MAIN FUNDAMENTAL RIGHTS

1) RIGHTS TO EQUALITY:

The right to equality is the principle that all individuals, regardless of their background, are equal before the law. It encompasses several aspects

Act:Indian Constitution,Articles 14-18

Right to Equality (Articles 14-18)
Purpose:
Ensures equality before the law and prohibits discrimination on grounds of religion, race, caste, sex, or place of birth. It provides equal protection of the law to all citizens.

Right to equality in fundamental rights has several uses and benefits for common people
Protection Against Discrimination:
Common people can use the right to equality to protect themselves from discrimination based on factors such as race, religion, caste, gender, or place of birth. It ensures that they are treated fairly and equally in various aspects of life.
Access to Opportunities:
This right ensures that individuals have equal access to educational institutions, public employment, and public places, regardless of their background. It allows common people to pursue their goals and aspirations without unfair barriers.
Legal Protection:
Common people can rely on the right to equality to seek legal protection. It guarantees that everyone is subject to the same laws and enjoys equal protection, preventing arbitrary actions by the government.
Social Inclusion
It promotes social inclusion by eradicating practices like untouchability and discriminatory titles. Common people benefit from a more inclusive and harmonious society where everyone is equal before the law.
Fair Treatment:
The right to equality ensures that common people are not subject to unjust
or biased treatment by government authorities, public institutions, or individuals.
It serves as a safeguard against unfair
actions.

2) Right to Freedom

This includes freedom of speech and expression, assembly, association, movement, residence, and practice of any profession or occupation.

Act: Indian Constitution, Articles 19-22
Purpose:
Guarantees freedom of speech and expression, assembly, association, movement, residence, and practice of any profession or occupation, subject to certain reasonable restrictions.
Right to Freedom in fundamental rights has several uses and benefits for common people
Expressing Opinions
Allows individuals to freely voice their opinions, ideas, and concerns about various matters, including government policies and societal issues.
Peaceful Assembly:
Enables citizens to gather together for peaceful protests, demonstrations, or meetings to advocate for change or raise awareness about specific issues.
Association and Organizing:
Permits individuals to form groups, associations, or unions to collectively work towards common goals, whether related to social, political, or economic issues.
Choosing Profession or Occupation:
Provides the freedom to select and pursue a profession or occupation that aligns with one's interests and skills.
Relevant Act:
The Right to Freedom is primarily governed by the Indian Constitution itself, specifically under Articles 19 to 22. These articles provide the legal framework for safeguarding these freedoms in India.


3) Right Against Exploitation

The "Right Against Exploitation" is one of the Fundamental Rights enshrined in the Indian Constitution. It primarily aims to protect individuals, particularly vulnerable groups, from various forms of exploitation and abuse. It aims to prevent the dehumanizing practice of forced labor and trafficking, safeguarding the dignity and freedom of individuals. It seeks to protect the physical and mental well-being of children, ensuring they have access to education and a safe childhood.

Act: Indian Constitution, Articles 23-24

Purpose:
Prohibits all forms of forced labor and trafficking and also prohibits the employment of children below 14 years in hazardous occupations.
Right against Exploitation in fundamental rights has several uses and benefits for common people
Protection from Forced Labor:
This right ensures that no individual can be compelled to work against their will, providing them with the freedom to choose their occupation.
Safeguarding Children's Rights
It prevents the exploitation of children by prohibiting their employment in dangerous or harmful work environments, thus ensuring their well-being and education.
These rights are essential for the well-being and dignity of individuals, and they contribute to building a more just and humane society.







4) Right to Freedom of Religion

The right to freedom of religion means that every individual has the right to practice and follow the religion of their choice. This right is enshrined in articles 25-28 of the Indian constitution. The "Right to Freedom of Religion" is a fundamental right that grants individuals the freedom to profess, practice, and propagate any religion of their choice.

Act: Indian Constitution, Articles 25-28

Purpose:
Ensures freedom of conscience and the right to profess, practice, and propagate any religion. It guarantees the freedom of religious institutions to manage their own affairs.
Right to Freedom of Religion in fundamental rights has several uses and benefits for common people:
Freedom of Worship:
Individuals can freely practice their religion, including participating in religious ceremonies, rituals, and worship.
Choice of Religion:
People have the freedom to choose their religion or belief system without coercion or discrimination.
Establishment of Religious Institutions:
Communities have the right to establish and manage religious institutions, such as temples, mosques, churches, gurdwaras, etc.
Religious Neutrality of the State:
The state cannot favor or promote any particular religion, ensuring that citizens are free to practice their faith without state interference.

The Right to Freedom of Religion is essential for preserving religious pluralism, diversity, and individual autonomy in a democratic society. It allows citizens to follow their own spiritual paths and participate in religious practices according to their beliefs.


5) Cultural and Education Rights

Cultural and Educational Rights are a set of Fundamental Rights enshrined in the Indian Constitution (Articles 29 and 30) that aim to safeguard the rights of religious and linguistic minorities to preserve their distinct culture, language, and educational institutions.

Act: Indian Constitution, Articles 29-30

Purpose:
Cultural and Educational Rights: These rights protect the interests of minorities to conserve their culture, language, and education.
Right to Freedom of Religion in fundamental rights has several uses and benefits for common people:
Preservation of Cultural Identity:
Cultural and educational rights help in preserving the unique cultural and linguistic identities of minority communities.
Access to Education with Cultural Sensitivity:
It allows minority communities to establish educational institutions that cater to their specific cultural and linguistic needs, ensuring a more inclusive and sensitive educational environment.
Empowerment of Minority Communities:
By granting them the right to establish and administer their own educational institutions, these rights empower minority communities to take charge of their own educational development.
Promotion of Diversity and Pluralism:
These rights contribute to the overall diversity and pluralism of Indian society by recognizing and protecting the rights of various cultural and linguistic groups.

Cultural and Educational Rights are essential for promoting inclusivity, diversity, and the overall well-being of minority communities. They contribute to a more equitable and inclusive educational system
that respects and accommodates the diverse cultural fabric of the nation



6) Right to Property:

The "Right to Property" was initially a Fundamental Right in the Indian Constitution but has undergone significant changes over the years

Act: Indian Constitution, Articles 31

Purpose:
Property rights are created in order to regulate the traffic between legal persons, and in that function they affect the way in which cities are formed and used. Urban planning necessarily restricts the way in which people may use their property rights, and for that reason is subject to political and economic analysis.
Right to Freedom of Religion in fundamental rights has several uses and benefits for common people:
Protection of Property Rights:
While it is no longer a Fundamental Right, Article 300A ensures that individuals cannot be arbitrarily deprived of their property. Any acquisition or deprivation must be in accordance with established legal procedures.
Legal Recourse:
If someone's property rights are violated, they have the legal recourse to challenge such actions in a court of law.
Ensuring Fair Compensation:
In case of compulsory acquisition of property by the government, individuals are entitled to fair compensation as per established legal procedures.
Providing Clarity on Property Rights:
Article 300A provides clarity on property rights, specifying that any deprivation must be in accordance with law, which helps in maintaining legal order and protecting citizens' interests.
It's important to note that the Right to Property, as originally conceived, was modified due to socio-economic considerations and changing legal interpretations. While it is no longer a Fundamental Right, the legal framework ensures that property rights are protected under established legal procedures.



7) Right To Constitutional Remedies

This allows citizens to move to the court if they believe their rights are violated.After that, the High Court or Supreme Court will issue the directives or orders to the government for the right enforcement.

Act: Indian Constitution, Articles 32

Purpose:
Constitution provides for legal remedies for the protection of these rights against their violation by the State or other institutions/individuals. It entitles the citizens of India to move the Supreme Court or High Courts for the enforcement of these rights. The State is forbidden from making any law that may conflict with the Fundamental Rights.
Right To Constitutional Remedies in fundamental rights has several uses and benefits for common people:
Access to Justice:
Article 32 provides individuals with a direct avenue to seek justice from the highest court of the land when they believe their Fundamental Rights have been violated.
Safeguarding Fundamental Rights:
It serves as a safeguard, ensuring that citizens can hold the government accountable if their rights, as outlined in the Constitution, are infringed upon.
Preventing Government Overreach:
Article 32 acts as a check on potential abuses of power by the government, helping to maintain a balance between state authority and individual rights.
Ensuring Rule of Law:
It reinforces the principle of the rule of law, where even the government is bound by legal constraints and can be challenged if it violates citizens' rights.




FUNDAMENTAL DUTIES

Fundamental Duties

Fundamental duties are a set of moral and civic obligations outlined in the Indian Constitution. They are aimed at promoting a sense of discipline, integrity, and responsibility among citizens. Here are some of the key fundamental duties for common people.
To abide by the Constitution and respect its ideals and institutions:
This means respecting the laws and principles that govern the country.
To cherish and follow the noble ideals that inspired our national struggle for freedom:
This emphasizes the importance of upholding the values that led to India's independence.
To uphold and protect the sovereignty, unity, and integrity of India:
This entails safeguarding the nation's territorial boundaries and unity.
To defend the country and render national service when called upon to do so:
This duty calls for citizens to contribute to the nation's defense and public service if required.
To promote harmony and the spirit of common brotherhood amongst all the people of India transcending religious, linguistic, and regional or sectional diversities:
This involves fostering a sense of unity and respect for diversity.
To renounce practices derogatory to the dignity of women:
his duty aims to promote gender equality and respect for women.
To value and preserve the rich heritage of our composite culture:
This duty encourages citizens to appreciate and protect India's diverse
cultural heritage. To protect and improve the natural environment
including forests, lakes, rivers and wildlife, and to have compassion
for living creatures:
This calls for responsible environmental stewardship and compassion towards animals
To develop the scientific temper, humanism, and the spirit of inquiry
and reform:
This emphasizes the importance of critical thinking, scientific inquiry, and a progressive
outlook.
To safeguard public property and to abjure violence:
This duty encourages citizens to respect public resources and resolve conflicts peacefully.

These fundamental duties are meant to guide citizens in contributing to the overall well-being and
progress of the nation. They serve as a reminder of the responsibilities that come with citizenship.
